Design in browser: what does it mean?

Yes to all the things. It encompases a lot of different practices.

Definition

Get your design to the browser as quickly as possible.

WHY?

  • The design will ultimately live online
  • Less troubleshooting later
  • Show clients real interactions
  • Reduces design time
  • Easier to use a mobile first approach
  • Reduce ambiguity!

Design for multiple devices

Graceful degradation vs. Progressive enhancement

Two ways of looking at design for the web.

Graceful degradation

Design for the biggest common denominator first, then “gracefully degrade” the experience for lesser devices (or browsers).

Leads to very lose definitions of "graceful" and "degradation."

Not cool, man.

We should never tell users how to best view the content.

Progressive enhancement

Start with the lowest “common denominator” then progressively enhance the experience for advanced devices.

Progressive enhancement pros

  • Focus is on the content
  • More forward-thinking
  • Works better for a mobile-first approach

Progressive enhancement & Design in browser.
Go together like PB&J.

*But they're not mutually exclusive.

What about mockups?

More and more designers, shops, agencies, are dumping mockups in favor of a hybrid deliverable

  • Style tiles
  • Wireframes*
  • Style guide

*Some advocate against showing wireframes to clients, only using wireframes internally, and showing clients a working prototype.

Moving away from pixel perfection

  • Websites don’t look the same on all browsers, devices
  • Mockups can give a false sense of “completeness” to the client
  • Mockups don’t communicate actual interactivity

Spend a lot of time hacking / tweaking

Embrace the freedom that comes with knowing things don't look the same everywhere.

Style tiles

Style tiles are an expression of a website through individual elements, presented together. They form a common language for the site.

So, like moodboards?

Not quite. Moodboards represent the thing, but are not the thing.

Style tiles define

  • Logo usage on the site
  • Typography
  • Imagery
  • Buttons
  • Look and feel of other elements (headers, footers, sidebar)

Problems style tiles solve

  • Make it pop
  • It should look like Apple
  • It should be like Facebook, but more like Yahoo

Style tiles use psychology to arrive at a look and feel

  • Identify stakeholders to provide input
  • Ask them to fill out a questionnaire about their brand
  • Identify trends in their answers
  • Create a visual design based on stakeholder feedback

Why this works

“The style tile process teases out the passion behind a brand, revealing nuggets of descriptive goodness all while connecting the client to the project.”

Source: A List Apart.

Why this works

  • Makes the client feel a part of the process
  • It gives you more actionable feedback
  • Reduces “bikeshedding” syndrome

Step 1: Identify stakeholders

  • Should be easy whether working on an internal project or with external clients, identify folks that are familiar enough with the brand and the goals of the project.
  • Try to get a good representative mix of brand representatives / advocates.
  • Send an email questionnaire or hold an in-person workshop.

Step 2: The questionnaire

  • If your brand was a car, what type of car would it be and why?
  • If your brand was a city, what type of city would it be and why?
  • If you website was a household appliance, it would be ___ because ___.

You can write your own, or you can find some online. Try to keep questions are general or relatable as possible.

Bad questions

If your brand was a font, what type of font would it be?

 

Why is this a bad question? This is too specific. Not all people are familiar with fonts. You may get stuck using these fonts in your design.

Bad questions

Stay away from comparing the site to existing sites. You want to arrive at a new visual language for the site.

 

Don’t get your clients stuck on looking at other sites. Otherwise, you’re back at “make it look like Apple.”

Step 3: Analyzing the questionnaire

If your brand was a city, what city would it be and why?

  • My brand would be like Portland, because it’s artistic and cares about the environment.
  • My brand would be Austin, because it embraces music and creativity and it’s laid back.
  • My brand would be like Berkley, because it’s socially responsible, thoughtful, and bohemian.

Step 4: Create the style!

Based on the feedback to the questionnaire, and the trends you’ve identified, you can now create style tiles to present to the client.

Rules of thumb

  • Present 2 or 3 variations. Keep it simple.
  • Client’s shouldn’t try to pick and choose.
  • Iterate
